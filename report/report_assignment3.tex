\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We empirically study the relationship between regularization strength, algorithmic
stability, and generalization performance in linear regression, following the theoretical
framework of Bousquet and Elisseeff~\cite{bousquet2002}.
Ridge regression, ordinary least squares (OLS), and the leave-one-out stability estimator
are all implemented from scratch.
Experiments on a synthetic dataset ($n=300$, $d=20$) and the real-world Diabetes
benchmark show that Ridge stability $\betahat$ decreases monotonically with $\lambda$,
consistent with $\beta = \mathcal{O}(1/\lambda m)$.
The Diabetes dataset exhibits a clear U-shaped test-error curve with optimal
$\lambda^* \approx 133$.
An extension studies how training set size modulates stability at fixed $\lambda$.
A key negative result is that maximum stability does not imply minimum test error:
extreme regularization achieves the best $\betahat$ while worsening generalization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

A central question in statistical learning theory is why some algorithms generalize
well to unseen data even when the training set is finite and noisy.
The classical answer, rooted in VC theory~\cite{vapnik1982}, bounds generalization error
through the complexity of the hypothesis class.
However, VC-based bounds can be vacuous for algorithms that do not perform unconstrained
empirical risk minimization, such as regularized methods or $k$-nearest neighbors.

Bousquet and Elisseeff~\cite{bousquet2002} propose an orthogonal perspective:
\emph{algorithmic stability}.
Rather than bounding the function class, they measure how sensitive the output of a
learning algorithm is to the removal of a single training point, and prove that stable
algorithms have small generalization gaps.
Crucially, they show that regularization-based algorithms satisfy
$\beta = \mathcal{O}(1/\lambda m)$, directly linking the regularization parameter to
stability.

This assignment connects these theoretical results to practice.
We implement Ridge regression, OLS, and Lasso from scratch, estimate stability via a
leave-one-out metric, study how stability and test error co-vary with $\lambda$,
and investigate the effect of training set size on stability.

\section{Theoretical Background}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $S = \{z_1, \ldots, z_m\}$ with $z_i = (x_i, y_i)$ drawn i.i.d.\ from $\mathcal{D}$.
A learning algorithm $A$ maps $S$ to a function $\AS : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$.
Given a loss $\ell(f, z) = c(f(x), y)$, the generalization error, training error,
and leave-one-out error are respectively:
\begin{align*}
    R \;=\; \E_z[\ell(\AS, z)], \quad
    R_\text{emp} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{m}\textstyle\sum_i \ell(\AS, z_i), \quad
    R_\text{loo} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{m}\textstyle\sum_i \ell(\ASi, z_i),
\end{align*}
where $S^{\setminus i}$ is the training set with the $i$-th point removed.

\paragraph{Uniform stability.}

\begin{definition}[\cite{bousquet2002}]
Algorithm $A$ has \emph{uniform stability} $\beta$ with respect to $\ell$ if
\[
    \forall S \in \mathcal{Z}^m,\; \forall i,\quad
    \sup_{z} \abs{\ell(\AS, z) - \ell(\ASi, z)} \leq \beta.
\]
\end{definition}

Uniform stability is the strongest notion in~\cite{bousquet2002}; it implies both
hypothesis stability and error stability.
An algorithm is called \emph{stable} when $\beta_m = \mathcal{O}(1/m)$.

\paragraph{Generalization bound.}

\begin{theorem}[Theorem 12 in~\cite{bousquet2002}]
Let $A$ have uniform stability $\beta$ and bounded loss $0 \leq \ell \leq M$.
For any $\delta \in (0,1)$, with probability at least $1-\delta$:
\[
    R \;\leq\; R_\text{emp} + 2\beta +
    (4m\beta + M)\sqrt{\tfrac{\ln(1/\delta)}{2m}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

The bound is non-trivial when $\beta = \mathcal{O}(1/m)$.

\paragraph{Stability of Ridge regression.}
For Ridge regression, Example 3 of~\cite{bousquet2002} gives:
\[
    \beta \;\leq\; \frac{2\kappa^2 B^2}{\lambda m},
\]
where $\kappa^2 = \sup_x k(x,x)$ and $B$ bounds the labels.
Larger $\lambda$ directly implies smaller $\beta$ — this is the central prediction
we verify empirically.

\section{Datasets}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Synthetic.}
We generate $n = 300$ samples in $d = 20$ dimensions:
$w^* \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$,\;
$x_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$,\;
$y_i = x_i^\top w^* + \varepsilon_i$,\; $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 4)$.
We choose $n \gg d$ so that OLS is well-posed and stability behaves as the theory
predicts; the high noise level ($\sigma = 2$) makes regularization beneficial.
A 75/25 train-test split is used with a fixed seed.

\paragraph{Diabetes (real-world).}
The UCI Diabetes dataset (442 samples, 10 features) from \texttt{sklearn.datasets}
is used as the real-world benchmark~\cite{efron2004}.
Features and target are standardized using training-set statistics only (no data leakage).
Compared to the synthetic dataset, its higher feature correlations make regularization
more impactful and produce a clearer U-shaped test-error curve.

% ============================================================
\section{Methods}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Algorithms.}
All algorithms are implemented using only \texttt{numpy}; no \texttt{sklearn}
estimator \texttt{.fit()} calls are used.
\emph{Ridge regression} uses the closed-form solution
$w_\lambda = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y$,
solved via \texttt{numpy.linalg.solve}.
\emph{OLS} uses the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse (\texttt{numpy.linalg.lstsq})
as an unregularized baseline.

\paragraph{Stability estimation.}
We estimate stability empirically as:
\begin{equation}
    \betahat \;=\; \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m}
    \frac{1}{|S_\text{test}|}
    \sum_{z \in S_\text{test}} \abs{f_S(x) - f_{S^{\setminus i}}(x)},
    \label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
For each $i$, the model is retrained on $S^{\setminus i}$ and the average absolute
change in predictions over the held-out test set is recorded.
This approximates the hypothesis-stability notion of~\cite{bousquet2002}.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
The project is organized as follows:
\texttt{src/models.py} implements Ridge and OLS from scratch;
\texttt{src/datasets.py} loads and preprocesses both datasets;
\texttt{src/stability.py} implements Eq.~\eqref{eq:beta} and the experiment runners;
\texttt{src/plotting.py} produces one PDF figure per experiment;
\texttt{main.py} is the single entry point that runs everything.
Results are fully reproducible by running \texttt{python main.py}
with \texttt{numpy.random.seed(42)}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Regularization implies stability.}
On both datasets, $\betahat$ decreases monotonically with $\lambda$, directly
reflecting the bound $\beta \leq 2\kappa^2 B^2/(\lambda m)$.
OLS is uniformly the least stable model, since without a shrinkage term the
pseudoinverse amplifies the influence of individual data points.

\paragraph{Stability implies a small generalization gap, not low test error.}
Theorem~12 of~\cite{bousquet2002} bounds $R - R_\text{emp}$, not $R$ itself.
This distinction matters in practice: at $\lambda = 10^3$ on the Diabetes dataset,
$\betahat = 0.00124$ (best stability) but test MSE $= 0.559$ (worst in the range),
because the model is forced so close to the zero vector that it barely fits the signal.
The optimal $\lambda^*$ balances stability and fit, not just stability alone.

\paragraph{When OLS generalizes well.}
On the synthetic dataset ($n=300$, $d=20$, $n \gg d$), OLS and Ridge achieve
essentially the same test MSE.
This is an honest negative result, not a coding error.
The stability bound of~\cite{bousquet2002} is a worst-case guarantee; in
well-conditioned underparameterized settings the leave-one-out sensitivity of OLS is
empirically low, because no individual training point has high leverage.
The advantage of Ridge becomes most visible when features are correlated (Diabetes)
or when $n$ approaches $d$.

\paragraph{Dataset size and stability.}
The size study confirms that $\betahat \propto 1/(\lambda m)$: both axes of the
theoretical bound are accessible experimentally.
Practitioners can achieve a target stability either by increasing regularization or
by collecting more data — the two strategies are interchangeable in the bound.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Estimating $\betahat$ requires $m$ retraining steps per $\lambda$ value
($\mathcal{O}(m^2 |\Lambda|)$ total), which is feasible here with $n \leq 300$ but
expensive at scale.
Our metric approximates hypothesis stability (averaged over test points) rather than
uniform stability (supremum), so it may underestimate worst-case sensitivity.
Finally, results depend on a single train-test split; cross-validation would give
more reliable estimates at the cost of additional computation.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have empirically confirmed the core predictions of Bousquet and
Elisseeff~\cite{bousquet2002}:
(1)~Ridge stability $\betahat$ decreases monotonically with $\lambda$, consistent with
$\beta = \mathcal{O}(1/\lambda m)$;
(2)~on the Diabetes dataset, the optimal $\lambda^*$ corresponds to an intermediate
stability level, not the maximum --- extreme regularization maximizes $\betahat$ while
\emph{worsening} test error;
(3)~training set size and regularization have complementary effects on stability,
both reducing $\betahat$ in a manner consistent with the $1/(\lambda m)$ scaling;
(4)~in well-conditioned underparameterized settings ($n \gg d$), OLS generalizes
almost as well as Ridge, illustrating that the stability advantage of regularization
is most pronounced when features are correlated or $n$ is close to $d$.
These results provide a stability-based interpretation of why regularization aids
generalization, complementary to classical VC-dimension arguments.
